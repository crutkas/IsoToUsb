IsoToUsb — elevation focus shift

The left setup pane and right pipeline pane are treated as floating cards instead of flat columns. During setup, the left card becomes the higher/focal surface; once building starts, focus shifts to the pipeline card so the user's eye follows the work.

All variants keep the current Workshop structure intact: 280 px setup, flexible live log, 300 px pipeline, and the 72 px bottom action bar with status pill, progress, Refresh/Cancel/Start and caption row. The states can be driven by existing VM state such as IsBusy, Status, Result and IsResultVisible — no new binding concepts are required.

Accent · Light Accent · Dark Success Hover frames to exaggerate the active-card elevation/glow.
Concept A

Twin floating cards

Both side panes become literal floating cards. Setup lifts the input card with a deeper shadow and slight upward offset; Building and Done shift that same lift to the pipeline card.

Transition cues

  • 180 ms shadow ramp; active card translates up 5 px.
  • Inactive card keeps content fully readable but drops to a low fake shadow.
  • Done reuses the right-side focus with success green as the state accent.

Pros / cons

  • Best literal answer to 'which side is higher'; maps cleanly to ThemeShadow or layered Borders.
  • Low usability risk because nothing is dimmed or scaled.
  • Needs careful dark-theme shadow tuning so the inactive card does not disappear.

Concept B

Spotlight + dim

Both cards stay elevated, but the non-current side receives a translucent wash while the focal card keeps full saturation and a soft accent halo. The eye shift is more theatrical than structural.

Transition cues

  • Opacity drops to about 68% on the inactive side; saturation reduces slightly.
  • Active card gains a subtle 8-13 px accent glow instead of growing.
  • Leaving Building removes the wash from inputs and moves success glow to pipeline.

Pros / cons

  • Very easy to implement with Opacity and a faked overlay Border.
  • Strong focus cue for quick scanning.
  • Usability concern: dimmed setup card must remain readable for confirming ISO/USB details.

Concept C

Stacked z-order

The focal side visually overlaps a sliver of the sunken log, implying it is physically in front. During setup, left tucks over the log; during build, right tucks over it.

Transition cues

  • Active card moves 7 px toward the log and rises 4 px.
  • Negative margin creates a controlled 22 px overlap while preserving the same grid footprint.
  • Inactive side scales down only to 98.5% to avoid jarring row movement.

Pros / cons

  • Most spatial and memorable option; makes z-order legible without adding labels.
  • Implementation risk is moderate: overlap needs clipping discipline in XAML and scale can look janky on slow GPUs.
  • Log loses a small edge under the active card, so dense log readers may object.

Concept D

Accent-edge lift

Cards keep equal physical elevation, but the focal side receives an accent edge stripe, border tint and soft glow. It is the most conservative way to communicate state-driven focus.

Transition cues

  • A 4 px accent stripe appears on the outside edge of the active card.
  • Border tint and glow swap from left in Setup to right in Building/Done.
  • No opacity, scale, or overlap changes; all content remains stable.

Pros / cons

  • Lowest implementation risk: BorderThickness/BorderBrush plus ThemeShadow or layered Border.
  • Very accessible and readable; avoids dimming important safety information.
  • Less literal than true elevation, so it may need a restrained shadow boost to satisfy the 'higher' brief.

Designer recommendation

Ship Concept A · Twin floating cards, with a small accent-edge borrowed from Concept D if needed. It most directly solves the user's plane complaint: the card that matters is literally higher, while the inactive side remains readable for safety checks.

Implementation is practical in WinUI 3 using ThemeShadow or a layered Border plus a ≤180 ms state transition. Avoid the full dimming and scale-heavy options unless user testing shows the focus shift still needs more emphasis.
