IsoToUsb — UX Explorations

Four distinct directions for the same six-phase pipeline (Validate · Mount · Wipe · Copy · Split WIM · Verify). Every frame is at production size (1140×750) so visual weight, density, and reading order can be judged honestly. Each concept is shown in both themes across three moments: idle (ready to start), building (mid-copy, 65 %), and done (success).

A deliberate constraint is applied throughout: no popping UX. Between idle → building → done, the spatial layout never reflows — status, progress and result occupy reserved slots so the eye stays anchored.

Concept A

Hero — One-shot canvas

A single, deeply centered card that treats "burn an ISO" like a one-shot creative action. Minimal chrome, generous whitespace, the log lives in a slide-out drawer for users who want it. The same composition serves all three states — only the centre slot changes.

Best for

First-time and once-a-year users. The kind of person who downloads an ISO, plugs in a stick, and wants a single obvious button. Reads as "appliance", not "tool".

Trade-offs

  • Risk of feeling under-powered to admins; log drawer must be discoverable.
  • Centred composition is the hardest to keep pop-free — fixed slot sizes are non-negotiable.
  • Errors must take over the centre slot fully (with the chips dimmed) or they get lost.

Concept B

Stepper — Numbered wizard

Five explicit steps (Source · Target · Review · Build · Done) presented as a numbered ribbon along the top. Each step owns a fixed-height body slot, so moving between steps swaps content without resizing the window. The Review step is the moment of commitment; the Build step is purely passive.

Best for

IT folks deploying many sticks who appreciate explicit confirmation. Also good as the "first-launch" walkthrough mode that more experienced users could collapse later.

Trade-offs

  • Step transitions are inherently page-changes — biggest pop risk if not animated carefully.
  • Three real choices stretched into five steps feels heavy for repeat use.
  • "Back" during a destructive build is meaningless — Build/Done steps must hide it.

Concept C

Workshop — Pro three-pane

A fixed three-column layout: inputs & settings on the left, the live log front-and-center, phase telemetry on the right. Every region keeps its exact footprint between states — only the inner content of each pane changes. The log is treated as a first-class citizen, not hidden behind a drawer.

Best for

Power users, sysadmins, and anyone who's been bitten by a silent burn. Reads as "tool", not "appliance" — like a build pipeline UI for storage.

Trade-offs

  • Density can intimidate first-time users; needs an "auto-collapse the log" affordance.
  • Three columns is dense at 1140 px — anything wider would breathe better.
  • Risk of the log scrolling fast enough to feel "busy" during Copy.

Concept D

Timeline — Vertical story

A vertical phase list with a fixed footprint sits at the top; a reserved "focus panel" below always occupies the same vertical slot and only swaps its inner content between idle, building and done. The eye never leaves the same two regions — pop is structurally impossible.

Best for

Confident, calm progress for long operations. Reads like a CI build view — the appropriate metaphor for a 7-minute destructive task. Works equally well for first-timers and admins.

Trade-offs

  • The fixed focus panel must be tall enough to hold "now copying X" without scrolling on a 248-char path.
  • Less visually striking on the done state — the celebration has to live in the same panel.
  • If a phase is skipped (e.g. no large WIM to split), the row stays but greys out — needs clear "skipped" affordance.

Side-by-side comparison

Each tile is a real frame at 62 % scale so the overall composition can be compared at a glance. Mid-build state is shown for every concept since that is where the personality of the design is most visible.

Recommendation

If we optimize for calm, anti-pop, and trust during a long-running destructive operation, the strongest direction is D · Timeline: the vertical phase list has a fixed footprint, growth only happens inside an already-reserved expanded panel, and the eye never has to search for "what changed". A close second is C · Workshop for power users who want the log front-and-center without sacrificing predictability — every region is a fixed grid cell that simply switches its inner content between states.

A · Hero is the most beautiful but the riskiest for "pop": a centered card naturally wants to shrink/grow with content and the transition between idle and building reads like a different screen. B · Stepper is honest but feels heavy for a 3-input task — each step transition is a page change, which is the most extreme form of pop.

If forced to ship one tomorrow: D · Timeline (default) with the Workshop log drawer as a "Show details" toggle. That combination keeps casual users in the calm timeline view and lets power users pull up the full log without a separate mode.
